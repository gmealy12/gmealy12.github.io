Introduction¶

League of Legends is currently the largest Multiplayer Online Battle Arena (MOBA) video game, developed by Riot Games. Each match is comprised of two teams of five players. Each team is tasked with destroying the Nexus of the opposing team, and the first to do such wins the game. The Nexus is located in the heart of each team's base, making it difficult to get to. Along with advantages arising from fighting other players, there are also neutral objectives on the map that either team may take. These neutral objectives are located somewhat centrally so each team has a relatively equal chance of taking them. Neutral objectives grant some type of buff or other unique advantage with the goal of favoring the team that takes said objective.

I chose this dataset because I play League of Legends and have always been curious about what the most important aspects of the game are, statistically. I chose to use data from Challenger matches because Challenger represents the top 200 players on a given server (in this case, North America). Since these players are ranked so highly, they will play the game more optimally than those in lower ranks. Analyzing this data will more accurately show the impacts of objectives and item choices on the outcome of the game.

In this tutorial, my goal is to show how raw data obtained from Riot's API can be processed and how to extract the data we care about. I want to show how different aspects of the game affect the outcome of the game, including gold difference, objectives taken, and even item choices.

Analyzing Gold Advantage of the Winning Team¶

One of the tricky parts of working with the data returned from the Riot API is that there are several nested JSONs within the main JSON returned, and extracting them from the main dataframe to their own dataframes for processing can sometimes be a pain.

In [6]:
match_data['Gold Difference'] = 0
for index, match in match_data.iterrows():
    #get player data from current match
    #the nested json was saved as a string, so we must use a parser to get the data into something pandas can read
    players = pd.DataFrame(ast.literal_eval(match['info.participants'][1:-1]))
    winning_gold = sum(players[players['win']]['goldEarned'])
    losing_gold = sum(players[players['win'] == False]['goldEarned'])
    match_data.loc[index, 'Gold Difference'] = winning_gold-losing_gold

With the ending gold advantage calculated for the winning team of each match, we can plot it so we can visualize how it varies with the total duration of the match.

In [7]:
x = match_data['info.gameDuration'] / 60
y = match_data['Gold Difference']
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12,9))

# calculate linear fit line
m, b = np.polyfit(x, y, 1)
plt.plot(x, m*x+b)
plt.xlabel('Game Duration (mins)')
plt.ylabel('Gold Advantage (winning team)')
plt.title('Gold Difference vs Duration of Game')
plt.show()

The linear fit line shows a downward trend as the total duration of the match increases. This means that as the game goes longer, there is less of an advantage between the two teams, meaning the game is closer. Intuitively, this makes sense because in games where one team gets a very large advantage early in the game, it is expected that they will end the game more quickly. However, in games where the gold is close, both teams are more evenly matched and therefore the game will typically take longer as one team is not strong enough to defeat the other team entirely.

Impact of Baron¶

Baron is a very important objective in League of Legends. Defeating Baron grants all champions on your team a buff that grants combat power as well as empowers your minions when you are nearby. Empowered minions make taking towers much easier and therefore gives you an advantage while you have the buff. Here we will calculate the win rate of taking Baron under different circumstances.

Impacts of Other Objectives¶

Inhibitors¶

Another objective that is very important to League of Legends are a team's inhibitors (each team has 3). Located in the team's base, taking these structures down causes super minions to spawn against the team that loses them. Super minions are significantly harder to kill than regular minions and are more effective at applying pressure against the team with the broken inhibitor. Let's calculate the win rate of the team that takes one of the other's inhibitors first.

Here we will perform a Linear Discriminant Analysis on objective variables to see if we can predict a win.

Now we have a dataframe containing independent variables (objectives) from each team which provide different advantages for that team in game. We also have another dataframe corresponding to whether the given team (matching row) won the match or not. We are now ready to perform a Linear Discriminant Analysis to see if we can accurately predict the outcome of a game given objectives taken.

We will determine how effective our analysis is by performing holdout validation on our dataset. The model will be trained using 80% of our data, and the remaining 20% will become the test data which our model will try to predict the outcome, and this prediction will be compared against the true outcome in order to determine how accurate the model is.

Multiple Holdout Sets¶

In [18]:
# initialize our x and y arrays for future plot
holdout_iter = [1]
model_accuracy = [percent_right]

In order to get a unique combination of training and test sets, we can use a random number generator to generate different seeds for the train-test splitter method every time we run it.

In [19]:
# set random seed so output is consistent
random.seed(43687962)
for i in range(1, 50):

    # Split data into a training and test set so we can perform holdout validation
    # This time, use random number generator to generate seed, therefore getting different sets of training and test data
    x_train, x_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(objective_info, win_lose, test_size = 0.2, random_state = random.randint(1, 9999999))

    # Format the data so it can be used by Scikit's LinearDiscriminantAnalysis
    x_train_arr = x_train[['baron', 'champion', 'dragon', 'inhibitor', 'riftHerald', 'tower']].to_numpy()
    y_train_arr = y_train[['win']].values.ravel()
    y_train_arr = np.array(y_train_arr, dtype=bool)

    clf = LinearDiscriminantAnalysis()
    clf.fit(x_train_arr, y_train_arr)

    win_pred = clf.predict(x_test[['baron', 'champion', 'dragon', 'inhibitor', 'riftHerald', 'tower']].to_numpy())
    win_pred = pd.DataFrame(win_pred).rename(columns={0:'prediction'})
    prediction_outcome = pd.concat([y_test.reset_index(drop=True), win_pred], axis=1)
    
    # calculate accuracy
    percent_right = sum(prediction_outcome['win'] == prediction_outcome['prediction']) / len(prediction_outcome.index) * 100

    # add to our x and y arrays
    model_accuracy.append(percent_right)
    holdout_iter.append(i + 1)

Each model prediction accuracy is saved, so we can plot it to visualize how well our models performed.

plt.ylabel('Model Accuracy (%)')

On average, the model we generate has about an 84-85% accuracy, so we have confirmed that the first model was not an outlier! These models are pretty accurate, but they could definitely be better. These models only take into account one team's objectives at a time and then attempts to determine whether that team won the game. A better model could take in the game state of both teams and evaluate both of their objectives taken together. Then, it would determine the winner and loser of the two. This way, the model could evaluate relative advantages of one team over another, resulting in more accurate predictions.

ADC Itemization¶

Items are a core part of League of Legends. As players earn gold (via farming minions, champion kills/assists, turret kills, etc), they spend the gold on items which provide stats and benefits that augment their champion and playstyle, making them stronger. With the item rework at the start of Season 11, completed items were classified into two main categories, Mythic and Legendary; the former being significantly stronger than the latter. The catch is each player can only build one Mythic item per game. As an Attack Damage Carry (ADC) player, I have always wondered what the most effective mythic item to build is. Traditional ADCs typically build one of three Mythic items: Kraken Slayer, Galeforce, or Shieldbow. Kraken Slayer grants the user an empowered attack every 3rd attack which does bonus damage; Galeforce grants the user a dash, allowing them to reposition quickly or escape an attacker; and Shieldbow gives the user a large shield for a short time when they take a lot of damage, increasing the champion's durability.

This plot is very interesting, as we can see how certain champions may synergize better with one Mythic item over another. For example, Miss Fortune has higher win rates when building both Galeforce and Kraken Slayer over building Shieldbow. Miss Fortune's damage is more burst-focused, and therefore she benefits from having the extra burst damage from empowered Kraken Slayer auto attacks and from the Galeforce execute dash-projectiles. Another champion to look at is Ashe. By default, Ashe does not do a lot of damage compared to most other ADCs. However, Ashe has a lot more utility at her disposal than most other ADCs, allowing her to set her team up for success. Because she does not do a lot of damage on her own, she benefits heavily from building Mythics which allow her to do more damage, covering her biggest weakness. Shieldbow is not very useful on Ashe because staying alive longer in a fight does not matter when you do not do enough damage to be a threat.

The flaw with this map is it may misrepresent some champions, as the sample size for many of the champions shown is quite small. The meta also effects how this plot looks. During any given meta, roughly 4-5 ADCs are played at a significantly higher rate than the other ADCs. In this meta, Jinx has a very large sample size because she is in meta on the current patch, while a champion like KogMaw only has a few games played because they are not in meta. Another misrepresentation in this data is known optimal builds on specific champions. For example, Vayne has a massive amount of damage built into her kit (champion abilities) by default. Because of this, almost everyone builds Shieldbow on her because it helps you stay alive longer and she does not benefit much from the extra damage the other Mythics would grant. However, looking at this plot, you would think that you should be building Galeforce on Vayne because it is a higher win rate, when in reality those are most likely outlier games where Vayne's team happen to win.

Item Pick Rates¶

Now that we acknowledged possible flaws in this plot, lets make a new plot to show the play rate of each Mythic. Challenger players are good for a reason, so they must be making mostly optimal decisions with their item picks, right?

In [28]:
adc_items['Total Plays'] = adc_items['Kraken Slayer Total'] + adc_items['Galeforce Total'] + adc_items['Shieldbow Total']
adc_items['Kraken Slayer Pick Rate'] = adc_items['Kraken Slayer Total'] / adc_items['Total Plays']
adc_items['Galeforce Pick Rate'] = adc_items['Galeforce Total'] / adc_items['Total Plays']
adc_items['Shieldbow Pick Rate'] = adc_items['Shieldbow Total'] / adc_items['Total Plays']

adc_picks = adc_items[['Champion Name', 'Kraken Slayer Pick Rate', 'Galeforce Pick Rate', 'Shieldbow Pick Rate']].copy()
adc_picks.rename(columns={'Champion Name':'Champion', 'Kraken Slayer Pick Rate':'Kraken Slayer', 'Galeforce Pick Rate':'Galeforce', 'Shieldbow Pick Rate':'Shieldbow'}, inplace=True)

adc_picks = pd.melt(adc_picks, id_vars='Champion', var_name='Item', value_name='Pick Rate')

cmap = sns.diverging_palette(220, 10, as_cmap=True)
pivot = adc_picks.pivot(index='Item', columns='Champion', values='Pick Rate')
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12,9))
sns.heatmap(pivot, cmap=cmap, square=True, linewidths=.5)
plt.title('ADC Mythic Item Pick Rates')
plt.show()

This plot gives us a better idea of what top players are building on each ADC and outlines the meta a bit more clearly. Using both this plot as well as the win rate plot we can make more informed decisions on which Mythic is most optimal to choose. If there are several Mythics with similar pick rates, it is most likely a game-dependant choice to make.

Conclusion¶

While I made in-depth analyses into specific parts of League of Legends in this tutorial, this data will soon be obsolete as new patches come out and the meta shifts. Luckily, the code in this tutorial can be re-ran on new patches with new data and can then provide new insights.

I hope this tutorial provided insight into not only processing data from League of Legends, but processing large amounts of data as a whole. I showed data collection, cleaning and wrangling the data, visualizing it, and a bit of Machine Learning predictions. The data science pipeline can sometimes be intimidating, but breaking it down into managable chunks is key.

Riot's developer portal hosts significantly more APIs than the ones I used in this tutorial, so there is an entire sea of data that can be pulled related to League of Legends as well as other Riot Games (VALORANT, Legends of Runterra, etc.). Even within League of Legends data, I did not even scratch the surface of what can be done; match data can be pulled with a timeline of all events that happened during a match and when, even giving locational data. Also, I only used Challenger data whereas data can be pulled from every single player on a given server. I hope this inspires others to make other interesting analyses or applications. Thanks for reading!
